How far off are these maps from an average map?
In order to determine how gerrymandered a map is, we have to quantitate something. We choose to look how each variable differred between the different district. Figure 1 show a made-up state with six districts. The map on the left plot the % of people in each district with a college degree. Each district has similar values and is an example of spreading 'cracking' a variable between the districts. To get an idea of how bad a map is gerrymandered, we looked at the frequency histogram of the Packing/Cracking Index for each variable. The Packing/Cracking Index (PCI) is the co-efficient of variation for a variable in a map. For instance, if the % of people with a college degree is similar in all 59 districts, then the PCI will be low. A low PCI means that the variable (ie. % of people with college degree in a district) has been spread across the districts (ie. Cracking). If the % of people with a college degree varies greatly between all 59 districts (ie. some districts have a high % of people with a college degree while other districts have a low % of people with a college degree), then the PCI will be high.
